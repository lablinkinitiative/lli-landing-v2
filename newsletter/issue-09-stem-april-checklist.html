Your STEM Action Checklist for the Next 30 Days

April deadlines, summer prep, and exactly what to do right now — whether you got accepted, rejected, or are still waiting.

This Week's Focus

The April Inflection Point

Late March is one of the most consequential moments in the STEM student calendar. Summer program decisions are landing in inboxes. Some students got in. Some didn't. Some are still waiting. And almost everyone is trying to figure out what to do next.

This issue is your playbook for the next 30 days — organized by where you are right now.

Scenario 1

You Got Accepted — Now What?

Congratulations. But acceptance is just the start. Here's what to do in the next two weeks:

Within 48 hours

  • Formally accept the offer in writing (email, portal, or both)
  • Decline any programs you're not taking — do this promptly, it opens spots for waitlisted students
  • Mark your start date on a calendar and count back 4 weeks for prep time

Within 2 weeks

  • Confirm housing — know whether the program provides it or you arrange your own
  • Ask about stipend logistics: how is it paid? W-2 or 1099? When does the first payment arrive?
  • Request the name of your research supervisor or PI — reach out with a brief professional email
  • Ask if there are onboarding materials to review before you arrive (protocols, safety training, papers)
  • Update your LabLink CDP profile with the program — your gap analysis will recalibrate

First-gen note: Many programs will reimburse travel expenses, but you often have to pay upfront and submit receipts. Ask specifically: "Is there travel reimbursement, and what's the process?" Don't assume — and don't let upfront costs be a barrier. Programs want you there.

Scenario 2

You Got Rejected — Here's the Real Situation

A rejection from a competitive STEM program is not a verdict on your potential. NSF REU programs routinely have 5–10% acceptance rates. DOE SULI accepted 1,600 students out of 20,000+ applications last cycle. These rejections are about probability, not quality.

What to do this week

  • Email the program coordinator and ask for any feedback on your application (many will respond)
  • Look at fall or spring cycle programs — DOE SULI, NIH, and several REUs have non-summer tracks
  • Check the LabLink Internship Portal for industry and nonprofit programs with rolling deadlines
  • Talk to a professor at your own institution — campus research opportunities don't require applications

April programs still open

Not everything has a January deadline. Opportunities actively accepting applications in April include:

  • DOE SULI Fall 2026 — applications typically open May 1 for August start
  • Industry internships — Amazon, Google, and biotech companies hire on a rolling basis through May
  • Campus-based research — walk into your department's research coordinator office, not just online portals
  • Community college bridge programs — LSAMP, McNair, and similar programs often have spring enrollment
Scenario 3

You're Still Waiting — How to Use This Time

Decisions from some programs don't arrive until April or even May. Use this window strategically:

  • Complete your LabLink CDP profile to 80%+ — your gap analysis will show you exactly what skills to build
  • Take one online course that aligns with your target research area (Coursera, edX, MIT OpenCourseWare)
  • Read 2–3 recent papers from the lab where you applied — you'll stand out immediately if accepted
  • Apply to 2 more programs with later deadlines — keep your options open
For Everyone

Skills to Build Before Summer Starts

Whether you have a placement or not, April is prime time to close skill gaps. Here's what research mentors say matters most:

Universal lab skills

  • Lab notebook discipline — know the difference between a lab notebook and a scratch pad; learn to write reproducible entries
  • Safety certifications — many labs require completion before day one; ask if there are online modules you can complete in advance
  • Data organization — learn a consistent file naming convention and folder structure before you generate any data

Computational skills (for any STEM field)

  • Python basics — even a 2-hour intro puts you ahead in most wet labs
  • Excel/Sheets proficiency — data analysis starts here for most students
  • Git basics — version control is used in computational biology, chemistry, and physics alike

Soft skills that are actually hard

  • Email etiquette with PIs — brief, specific, no overexplaining
  • How to ask questions effectively ("I tried X, it didn't work because Y, should I try Z?")
  • Presenting data verbally — practice describing a graph or result in two sentences

Equity & Inclusion Corner

Navigating Your First Paid Research Stipend

For many first-generation college students, a summer research stipend is the first substantial check they've ever received as a professional. Some practical things that no one tells you:

  • It's usually taxable income. Set aside 15–20% if your program pays via 1099 (not W-2). Some programs withhold automatically; ask.
  • Housing stipends ≠ free housing. If you receive a housing allowance, you still need to find and pay for housing upfront in most cases. Plan for a deposit plus first month.
  • Your PI is not HR. If you have questions about stipend payment timelines, ask the program coordinator — not your research mentor.
  • Academic credit rules vary. Check with your home institution before assuming you can earn credit for the experience. Some programs require pre-approval paperwork in March.

These logistics shouldn't derail an opportunity. The programs exist to be accessible — they just don't always explain the details clearly up front. Ask early, ask directly, and remember that program staff have answered these questions many times before.
