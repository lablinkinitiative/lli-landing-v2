The Three Programs Every STEM Student Should Know

If you're a college student in STEM and you haven't heard of NSF REU, DOE SULI, or NIH SIP — this issue is for you. These three federal research programs collectively place thousands of students in paid, prestigious research positions every year. They're competitive, but not as out-of-reach as most students assume.

And they pay. Well.

NSF Research Experience for Undergraduates (REU)

The National Science Foundation's REU program funds over 700 REU Sites at universities across the country, each focused on a specific research area — from materials science to marine biology to computer security.

  • Duration: 10 weeks (typically June–August)
  • Stipend: ~$6,000 for the summer (varies by site)
  • Housing: Typically provided or subsidized
  • Locations: 700+ universities across the US
  • Deadline: January–February (varies by site)

Who's eligible: Undergraduate students at any US institution, including community colleges. You do not need to be enrolled at the university hosting the REU.

Application strategy: Use the NSF REU search tool at nsf.gov/crssprgm/reu to find all active sites. Apply to 8–12 programs — acceptance rates vary widely, and you won't know which ones are oversubscribed until you try. Focus your personal statement on genuine scientific curiosity, not just career goals.

DOE Science Undergraduate Laboratory Internship (SULI)

The Department of Energy's SULI program places students at 16 national laboratories — Argonne, Oak Ridge, Lawrence Berkeley, SLAC, Fermilab, and more. These are some of the most advanced research facilities in the world.

  • Duration: 10 weeks (summer) or 16 weeks (fall/spring)
  • Stipend: $600–$800/week (about $6,000–$12,800 total)
  • Locations: Any of 16 DOE national laboratories
  • Deadline: January 10 (summer cycle)

What makes SULI unique: You work alongside PhD-level scientists on active research projects. It's not a tour or an observation experience — you contribute to real science with real data. SULI alumni have extraordinarily strong graduate school application outcomes.

Application tip: Apply at science.osti.gov/wdts/suli and indicate laboratory and research area preferences. Specificity matters: the more clearly you articulate why you want to work on a particular type of research, the stronger your application reads.

NIH Summer Internship Program (SIP)

The National Institutes of Health SIP places students at NIH's own research campuses — primarily in Bethesda, Maryland, though some remote options exist. Note: the summer cycle deadline is March 1, earlier than NSF and DOE.

  • Duration: 8–10 weeks (summer) or year-round options
  • Rate: $20+/hour
  • Focus: Biomedical research
  • Deadline: March 1 (summer) — check for fall cycle

What makes NIH unique: You're working at the agency that funds most of America's health research. The professional network is extraordinary. NIH SIP alumni frequently go on to medical school, PhD programs, and careers in pharmaceutical research and public health.

Equity & Inclusion Corner

The Myth of the Elite STEM Student

A persistent myth: "These programs are for elite university students."

The reality: NSF, DOE, and NIH all have explicit diversity mandates. Programs receive additional funding for successfully placing underrepresented students, community college students, and first-generation students. You are not competing at a disadvantage — in many programs, your background is a strength that distinguishes your application.

The students who miss out on these opportunities aren't the ones who applied and weren't selected. They're the ones who never applied because they assumed they wouldn't get in.
