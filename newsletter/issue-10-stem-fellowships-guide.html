Technical Deep-Dive

The Three Programs Every STEM Student Should Know

If you’re a college student in STEM and you haven’t heard of NSF REU, DOE SULI, or NIH SIP — this issue is for you. These three federal research programs collectively place thousands of students in paid, prestigious research positions every year. They’re competitive, but not as out-of-reach as most students assume. And they pay.

NSF Research Experience for Undergraduates (REU)

The NSF REU program funds over 700 Sites at universities across the country, each focused on a specific research area — from materials science to marine biology to computer security.

Duration: 10 weeks (June–August)  |  Stipend: ~$6,000  |  Housing: Typically provided  |  Locations: 700+ universities

Eligibility: Undergraduates at any US institution, including community colleges. You do NOT need to be enrolled at the university hosting the REU. Apply to 8–12 programs — acceptance rates vary widely.

DOE Science Undergraduate Laboratory Internship (SULI)

The DOE SULI program places students at 16 national laboratories — Argonne, Oak Ridge, Lawrence Berkeley, SLAC, and more. These are some of the most advanced research facilities in the world.

Duration: 10–16 weeks  |  Stipend: $600–800/week  |  Locations: 16 DOE national labs

You work alongside PhD-level scientists on active research projects. SULI alumni have extraordinarily strong graduate school application outcomes. Apply at science.osti.gov/wdts/suli.

NIH Summer Internship Program (SIP)

The NIH SIP places students at NIH’s own research campuses — primarily Bethesda, Maryland. The deadline is March 1 — earlier than NSF and DOE.

Duration: 8–10 weeks  |  Rate: $20+/hour  |  Focus: Biomedical research

Equity & Inclusion Corner

The Myth of the Elite STEM Student

A persistent myth: “These programs are for elite university students.” The reality: NSF, DOE, and NIH all have explicit diversity mandates. Community college students, first-generation students, and underrepresented students are actively recruited.

The students who miss out on these opportunities aren’t the ones who applied and weren’t selected. They’re the ones who never applied because they assumed they wouldn’t get in. Apply anyway.
