How to Land Your First Research Experience (Even If You Think You're Not Ready)

Spring is here — and so is the biggest research application window of the year. Stop waiting for more experience. Research is how you get it.

If you've been telling yourself you'll apply "when you have more experience" — this is your sign to stop waiting.

Research experience is how you get more experience. Here's how to start.

Where to Look

The Three Federal Programs Every STEM Student Should Know

Most students never apply to federal research programs because they don't know they exist. Here are three you should know:

NSF REU — Research Experiences for Undergraduates

  • ~700 programs across every STEM field
  • 10-week summer program, ~$6,000 stipend + housing
  • Open to undergrads at any institution, including community colleges
  • Most deadlines: February–March for summer
  • Apply at: nsf.gov/crssprgm/reu

DOE SULI — Science Undergraduate Laboratory Internships

  • 16 national labs (Argonne, Lawrence Berkeley, Oak Ridge, and more)
  • 10–16 weeks, $600–800/week stipend
  • One of the best pathways into DOE lab careers
  • Apply at: science.osti.gov/wdts/suli

NIH SIP — Summer Internship Program

  • Year-round availability, not just summer
  • $20+/hour, hybrid and remote options available
  • Bethesda, MD main campus plus satellite locations
  • Apply at: training.nih.gov/programs/sip

All three programs are explicitly open to first-generation students and community college transfers.

Cold Email Template

The Cold Email That Actually Works

Many of the best research opportunities never get posted publicly — you have to ask. Here's a template that works:

Subject: Undergraduate Research Inquiry — [Your Field]

Dear Dr. [Name],

I'm a [year] studying [major] at [institution]. I came across your work on [specific paper or project] and found your approach to [specific topic] genuinely interesting.

I'm looking for a research opportunity this summer and would love to contribute to your lab. I have experience with [1-2 relevant skills], and I'm eager to learn [specific technique or area].

Would you have 15 minutes to speak, or is there an application process I should follow?

Thank you for your time,
[Your name]

Key principles: be specific (mention their actual work), be brief, offer value, and make the ask clear.

Resume Tips

Your Research Resume Checklist

A research resume is different from a job resume. Prioritize:

  • Research projects first (even class projects count)
  • Technical skills (lab techniques, software, languages)
  • Coursework relevant to the position
  • GPA (include if 3.0+)
  • One strong mentor reference who knows your work

Keep it to one page. Researchers are busy.

Equity & Inclusion Corner

You Belong in a Research Lab

First-generation students often self-select out of research programs before applying, assuming these opportunities are "for other people." They're not.

NSF REU, DOE SULI, and NIH SIP were all designed with access in mind. Many explicitly prioritize students from underrepresented institutions and backgrounds. The main barrier is awareness — which is why LabLink exists.

If you're a first-gen student reading this: you belong in a research lab. Apply anyway.
