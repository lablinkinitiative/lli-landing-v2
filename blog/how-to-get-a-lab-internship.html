How to Get a Lab Internship: A Student's Practical Guide

Getting your first lab internship is one of the most valuable things you can do as a STEM student. It's also one of the most confusing — because most programs don't advertise the way regular jobs do, and the unwritten rules aren't obvious unless you already know them.

This guide gives you the practical playbook for finding, applying to, and landing lab internships — whether you're in high school, early college, or switching into a STEM field.

Why Lab Experience Matters More Than You Think

A lab internship does things that no class can:

  • Exposes you to real science — where things don't work, hypotheses fail, and you have to troubleshoot
  • Builds technical skills — pipetting, data analysis, equipment operation, safety protocols
  • Gives you mentorship — researchers who can write recommendation letters and open doors
  • Confirms (or redirects) your interest — many students discover they love a field; others find they want to pivot
  • Signals commitment to admissions and employers — hands-on research experience is a strong differentiator

For students who want to go into medicine, graduate research, biotech, or engineering, lab experience isn't optional — it's table stakes.

Know What You're Looking For

Before you start reaching out, get clear on what kind of experience you want.

Research vs. applied lab work

  • Academic research labs (at universities or research institutes) focus on generating new knowledge. Work is open-ended, often slow, and scientifically rigorous. Great preparation for graduate school or research careers.
  • Industry labs (biotech, pharma, medical devices) focus on developing products and solving defined problems. Work is more structured, faster-paced, and commercially oriented. Great preparation for industry careers.
  • Clinical or hospital labs process samples and support diagnosis. Good for pre-med students who want exposure to healthcare settings.

What level are you?

  • High school students: Focus on high school research programs, university summer programs (many are specifically designed for pre-college students), and science fair research. Most research labs at universities are not set up to host high schoolers independently, so targeted programs matter.
  • Undergraduates: The full range opens up — REUs, faculty lab positions, industry internships, and summer programs.
  • Career changers or non-traditional students: Emphasize transferable skills and consider structured programs that bridge into research.

Find Opportunities

Most lab internships are never posted publicly. Here's how to find them.

Formal programs — start here

These programs are specifically designed to place students in labs:

  • NSF REU programs — 10-week paid summer research positions at universities across the US. Hundreds of programs, many focused on specific fields. Apply through NSF's REU site search. Highly competitive; apply to 5-10 programs.
  • NIH Undergraduate Scholarship Program (UGSP) — Paid scholarships plus summer research at NIH. Competitive.
  • HHMI Summer Research Programs — Various programs at HHMI-affiliated institutions for undergraduates.
  • University-specific programs — Many research universities run their own summer programs. Search "[university name] summer research undergraduate" or "high school research program."
  • Science fair programs — Intel ISEF, Siemens, regional fairs. These often come with mentorship from working researchers.

LabLink Initiative intern listings — Check intern.lablinkinitiative.org for curated opportunities matched to students who might not otherwise find them.

Cold outreach to researchers

This is how most lab positions are actually filled. It requires initiative, but it works.

  1. Find researchers doing work you're interested in (university faculty pages, Google Scholar, PubMed)
  2. Read one or two of their recent papers to understand what they're working on
  3. Email a short, specific message expressing genuine interest in their research and asking if they have room for a student
What works
  • Specific reference to their actual research
  • Clear statement of your skills and experience level
  • Explicit statement of what you're looking for and when
  • Brevity — 3-4 paragraphs maximum
What doesn't work
  • Generic "I'm passionate about science" messages
  • Asking what they research (look it up first)
  • Attaching a 4-page CV when a 1-page resume would do
  • Following up more than twice

Send 10-20 of these. Expect maybe 2-3 responses. That's normal.

Your network

Tell people you're looking. Tell your science teachers, professors, family friends, and anyone you know who works in research. A warm introduction to a lab PI (principal investigator) is dramatically more effective than cold email.

Prepare Your Materials

Resume/CV

For a student with limited experience, focus on:

  • Relevant coursework (especially lab courses)
  • Any previous research experience (even a class project)
  • Technical skills (software, equipment, techniques you know)
  • Academic achievements (GPA, honors, relevant competitions)

Keep it to one page. Make it clean and readable. Proofread everything.

Cover letter / email

Your initial email to a researcher is your cover letter. Keep it short, specific, and genuine. Don't use a template that looks like a template.

For formal program applications, follow their specific guidelines — many ask for essays, research statements, or letters of recommendation. Plan ahead; these take time to assemble.

Letters of recommendation

For formal programs, you'll need letters. Ask science teachers or professors who know your work well. Give them at least 3-4 weeks notice and provide them with your resume, the program description, and any relevant context about why you want that particular experience.

The Application Process

Timeline matters

Most summer programs have January-February deadlines for summer positions. If you're targeting the summer, start your search in October-November. This gives you time to research programs and labs, draft materials, get letters of recommendation, and revise and submit.

Cold outreach to labs can happen year-round, but for summer positions, reaching out by February gives labs time to plan.

Apply broadly

For competitive formal programs (REUs, NIH, etc.), apply to at least 5-10 programs. Acceptance rates can be low, and having multiple applications protects you from a bad outcome on any single one.

Follow up thoughtfully

If you don't hear back on a formal application after the stated timeline, a single polite follow-up is appropriate. For email outreach to researchers, one follow-up after 2 weeks is fine.

Make the Most of the Experience

Getting the internship is step one. Making it count is step two.

  • Show up prepared and on time. Lab culture takes punctuality seriously, and first impressions matter.
  • Ask good questions. The best thing you can do is engage genuinely with the work. Ask why things are done the way they're done. Ask what the lab is trying to figure out. Ask what the hardest part of the current project is.
  • Take notes. Keep a lab notebook from day one. Document protocols, results, observations, and questions. This habit will serve you for your entire scientific career.
  • Do your share of the unglamorous work. Every lab has routine tasks — washing glassware, making solutions, cleaning equipment. Do these without complaint. It builds trust and goodwill, and you'll learn more when the team trusts you enough to include you in real work.
  • Build relationships. The graduate students and postdocs who work beside you are your most accessible mentors. They've recently navigated the path you're on. Ask them about their experiences. Stay in touch after the internship ends.
  • Ask for a reference letter before you leave. At the end of your internship, ask your PI or supervisor if they'd be willing to serve as a reference for future opportunities. Don't wait until months later when they've forgotten the details of your work.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Waiting for a perfect opportunity. Any legitimate lab experience is valuable. Don't hold out for the highest-prestige program at the expense of gaining no experience.
  • Not following up at all. Many students email and then disappear when they don't hear back immediately. A single follow-up is expected and appropriate.
  • Being passive in the lab. The students who get the most out of internships are the ones who ask questions, take initiative, and engage actively. Sitting quietly hoping to absorb knowledge by osmosis doesn't work.
  • Not staying in touch. The researchers you work with are professional contacts for life. Keep them updated on your progress. These relationships matter enormously over a career.
  • Missing the deadline. Program deadlines are real. No one gives extensions because you didn't start your application in time.

Getting into a lab takes persistence. Most students who successfully land research internships reached out to 10, 20, or even 30 places before finding the right fit. That's not failure — that's the process. Start early, be specific, and keep going.
