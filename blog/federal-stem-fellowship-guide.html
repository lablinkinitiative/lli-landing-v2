NSF REU, DOE SULI, and NIH SIP: The Complete Guide for Students

Three federal agencies — the National Science Foundation, the Department of Energy, and the National Institutes of Health — run the largest and most prestigious paid research fellowship programs in the US. Together, they place thousands of students each year into real research positions at universities, national laboratories, and federal research campuses.

If you're a college student looking for summer research experience, these programs should be at the top of your list. This guide breaks down how each one works, who's eligible, and how to apply strategically across all three.

NSF REU: The Widest Net

The NSF Research Experiences for Undergraduates program funds over 700 individual sites hosted at universities and research institutions across the country. Each site is its own program with its own focus area, application process, and research projects.

What you'll do

REU participants are embedded in a research lab for 8–10 weeks, working directly under a faculty mentor on an ongoing research project. You'll have real responsibilities — running experiments, analyzing data, troubleshooting — not just shadowing. Most programs culminate in a research poster or presentation.

Finding the right REU site

NSF maintains a searchable database of all REU sites at nsf.gov. You can filter by research area (biology, chemistry, physics, engineering, computer science, social science, etc.) and by state. Pick programs where you're genuinely interested in the specific research being done — generic applications don't work well for REUs.

What makes a strong application

  • A personal statement tailored to the site's research — mention specific projects, faculty, or techniques you're interested in. Each site sees hundreds of generic "I love science" essays.
  • Strong academic record — most competitive sites look for a 3.0+ GPA, though this varies
  • 2–3 letters of recommendation — from science instructors who know your work, not just your grade
  • Any prior research experience — even a class project counts
Community college students: REUs explicitly welcome CC students, and many sites actively prioritize students from institutions with limited research infrastructure. Your CC status is not a disadvantage — it's often an advantage.

Strategy: Apply to 8–12 sites

REU acceptance rates at top sites can be below 5%. This isn't a reason to give up — it's a reason to apply broadly. Apply to 8–12 sites that genuinely interest you. The effort per application is similar once you've written your core materials, and each additional site dramatically increases your chances of an offer.

DOE SULI: Research at a National Laboratory

The Department of Energy's Science Undergraduate Laboratory Internship places students at one of 17 DOE national laboratories — including Argonne, Brookhaven, Lawrence Berkeley, Oak Ridge, Pacific Northwest, and others. These are some of the most advanced research facilities in the world, operating at scales universities cannot match.

What makes SULI different

National labs are not universities. They're organized around large-scale problems — energy, climate, nuclear science, computing, materials, bioscience — and they operate with a level of infrastructure and collaboration that's hard to find elsewhere. Spending a summer at a national lab exposes you to how large-scale science actually works.

How SULI works

You apply through the DOE Office of Science's central portal at science.osti.gov/wdts/suli. You can list up to three preferred laboratories. After acceptance, you'll be matched to a lab and a mentor based on your background and the lab's current projects. You'll work full-time for 10 weeks with a weekly stipend in the $600–$800 range (depending on academic year), plus housing assistance.

Two cycles

  • Summer (May–August) — main cycle; application opens in October, closes in January
  • Spring (January–May) — secondary cycle; application opens in August, closes in October

Strong candidates

SULI is competitive but values diversity of background. Students in physics, chemistry, biology, environmental science, engineering, and computer science all find placements. The program explicitly targets students from underrepresented groups and institutions without strong research infrastructure.

NIH SIP: Biomedical Research in Bethesda

The NIH Summer Internship Program places students at NIH's main campus in Bethesda, Maryland — the largest biomedical research campus in the world. If your interests are in biology, medicine, neuroscience, genetics, chemistry, or any adjacent field, this is one of the most prestigious experiences available.

The scale of NIH

NIH employs over 6,000 scientists across 27 institutes and centers. SIP participants are embedded within an NIH lab working directly under a principal investigator. The breadth of research is extraordinary — cancer biology, infectious disease, behavioral science, genomics, clinical translation, and much more.

Pay and logistics

NIH SIP pays hourly (typically $20+/hour, depending on year and background) for 8–10 weeks. Housing is not provided, and Bethesda is expensive — budget accordingly. However, NIH's public transit access (on Metro's Red Line) makes it feasible to live in more affordable parts of the DC area.

Application process

Applications open around November–December through NIH's centralized portal. You can indicate preferred institutes and research areas. Unlike REU, you don't apply to specific labs — you're matched after acceptance. Letters of recommendation and a research statement are required.

Remote opportunities: Some NIH SIP positions are available remotely or in hybrid format. If relocating to Bethesda is a barrier, filter for remote-eligible positions in the application.

How to Apply to All Three

These programs have overlapping application windows. Here's a realistic timeline for someone targeting all three for the following summer:

  • September–October: Research specific REU sites, DOE labs, and NIH institutes. Identify 10–15 programs that genuinely fit your interests. Start drafting your research statement and personal essay.
  • October–November: Ask for letters of recommendation — give faculty at least 4 weeks. Apply to DOE SULI (deadline January). Finalize your REU site list.
  • November–December: Apply to NIH SIP. Apply to early-deadline REU sites (some close in December).
  • January–March: Submit remaining REU applications as deadlines arrive. Follow up with recommenders to confirm letters are sent.
  • February–April: Interview invitations and offers. Evaluate and accept.

Materials you'll use across all three

  • Research statement / personal essay — customize per program, but build from a core draft
  • Resume or CV — 1 page for most REUs; NIH and DOE accept 2 pages for students with more experience
  • Unofficial transcript — required by all three
  • 2–3 letters of recommendation — most programs want letters from science faculty; ask the same recommenders for all programs (let them know the list)

What If You Don't Get In?

These programs are competitive. Rejection is common even for strong candidates, and it's rarely about you specifically — it's about fit, available projects, and the size of the applicant pool in a given year. A few things to keep in mind:

  • Apply again next year. Many students who are rejected the first time are accepted on a subsequent application. Your application will be stronger with another year of coursework and experience.
  • Look for university-based alternatives. Many research universities run their own funded summer programs that aren't part of NSF/DOE/NIH. Search "[university name] summer undergraduate research program."
  • Cold outreach to faculty works. If you're at a university, identifying a faculty member whose research interests you and emailing directly is a legitimate path to unpaid or paid research positions. The barrier is lower than a national competition.
  • Check the LabLink intern portal. We maintain a live database of STEM research opportunities including many that don't require US citizenship or have open rolling applications.

The students who land these fellowships aren't necessarily the most brilliant — they're the ones who started early, applied broadly, wrote specific essays, and followed through. The opportunity is there. The programs want to fill it with students like you.

If you're a community college student, a first-generation college student, or someone coming from a school without a strong research tradition, know this: all three programs have explicit missions to include you. Your background isn't a liability — it's something these programs are designed to address.
